Walking with Feet Tied to the Past: Childhood Exposure to the 1918 Influenza Pandemic and Later-Life Male Mortality

带着过去的束缚前行：童年期暴露于1918年流感大流行与晚年男性死亡率

Hamid Noghanibehambari, Jason Fletcher

环境与健康 未指明区域

Abstract

Many previous studies have used cross-cohort comparisons and a focus on in utero exposure to document the lingering effects of the 1918 influenza pandemic on survivors’ outcomes later in life. A subset has examined impacts on old-age health and mortality, finding mixed results. This paper contributes to the literature by extending the analysis to evaluate early-life and childhood exposure to the pandemic on old-age longevity along with a set of candidate mechanisms. We employ Social Security Administration death records linked to the full-count 1940 census and employ event-study and difference-in-difference regressions to compare longevity of cohorts with different age-at-exposure who were born in cities with high versus low exposure based on 1918 influenza-related mortality. Our treatment-on-treated calculations suggest that cohorts with age-at-exposure of 3-8 and 9-11 experience 6.4 and 11.2 months reductions in old age longevity; importantly, we note that these cohorts are often used as “controls” in the broader literature. While our main analysis focuses on males due to data constraints, supplementary results suggest smaller and less precise effects for females. An analysis of census data suggests reductions in health, education and socioeconomic scores as potential mechanisms.

中文摘要

许多先前的研究利用跨队列比较并聚焦于子宫内暴露，记录了1918年流感大流行对幸存者晚年结果的持续影响。其中一部分研究考察了对老年健康和死亡率的影响，结果不一。本文通过将分析扩展到评估早期生活和童年期暴露于大流行对老年寿命的影响以及一组候选机制，为文献做出贡献。我们利用与社会保障管理局死亡记录相关联的全量1940年人口普查数据，并采用事件研究和双重差分回归，比较了不同暴露年龄队列的寿命，这些队列基于1918年流感相关死亡率出生在高暴露与低暴露城市。我们的处理组平均处理效应计算表明，暴露年龄为3-8岁和9-11岁的队列，其老年寿命分别减少6.4个月和11.2个月；重要的是，我们注意到这些队列在更广泛的文献中常被用作“对照组”。虽然由于数据限制，我们的主要分析聚焦于男性，但补充结果表明对女性的影响较小且不精确。对人口普查数据的分析表明，健康、教育和社会经济得分的下降是潜在的机制。
