Randomized Regulation: The Impact of Minimum Quality Standards on Health Markets

随机化监管：最低质量标准对健康市场的影响

Guadalupe Bedoya, Jishnu Das, Amy Dolinger

环境与健康 肯尼亚

Abstract

We report results from the first randomization of a regulatory reform in the health sector. The reform established minimum quality standards for patient safety, an issue that has become increasingly salient following the Ebola and COVID-19 epidemics. In our experiment, all 1348 health facilities in three Kenyan counties were classifi ed into 273 markets, and the markets were then randomly allocated to treatment and control groups. Government inspectors visited health facilities and, depending on the results of their inspection, recommended closure or a timeline for improvements. The intervention increased compliance with patient safety measures in both public and private facilities (more so in the latter) and reallocated patients from private to public facilities without increasing out-of-pocket payments or decreasing facility use. In treated markets, improvements were equally marked throughout the quality distribution, consistent with a simple model of vertical differentiation in oligopolies. Our paper thus establishes the use of experimental techniques to study regulatory reforms and, in doing so, shows that minimum standards can improve quality across the board without adversely affecting utilization.

中文摘要

我们报告了卫生领域首次监管改革随机化实验的结果。该改革建立了患者安全的最低质量标准，这一问题在埃博拉和新冠疫情后日益突出。在我们的实验中，肯尼亚三个县的所有1348家医疗机构被划分为273个市场，然后这些市场被随机分配到处理组和对照组。政府检查员访问医疗机构，并根据检查结果建议关闭或设定改进时间表。该干预措施提高了公立和私立机构（后者更为显著）对患者安全措施的合规性，并将患者从私立机构重新分配到公立机构，同时未增加自付费用或减少机构使用率。在处理组市场中，质量分布各处的改善同样显著，这与寡头垄断中垂直差异化的简单模型一致。因此，我们的论文确立了使用实验技术研究监管改革的方法，并在此过程中表明，最低标准可以在不负面影响利用率的情况下全面改善质量。
