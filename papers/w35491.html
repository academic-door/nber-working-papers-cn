Retreating from Science: The Long-Run Effects of the 1970s U.S. Military Disinvestment from University Research

从科学领域撤退：1970年代美国军事部门对大学研究撤资的长期影响

Daniel P. Gross, Bhaven N. Sampat, Hansen Zhang

其他主题 美国

Abstract

Between 1945 and 1970, the U.S. Department of Defense was one of the country's largest funders of university research, and the most important in the physical sciences and engineering. Political pressures subsequently led to this funding declining nearly two-thirds by the mid-1970s and never fully recovering. We show that these cuts were felt across the U.S. research system, with ensuing declines in university scientists, PhD production, and research output. Early career scientists who were DoD-funded in 1970 were more likely than peers to leave universities, with many moving to industry. Though some became more likely to patent, in the aggregate, this appears to have reduced science-linked innovation in related technologies and compressed U.S. leadership in formerly DoD-driven fields. These effects extend to outcomes more closely related to military objectives: fewer PhDs entered the defense industrial base, and an initial increase in science-linked defense patenting in the most exposed technology areas gave way to a long-run decline.

中文摘要

1945年至1970年间，美国国防部是美国大学研究的最大资助方之一，并且在物理科学和工程领域最为重要。随后的政治压力导致这一资助到1970年代中期下降了近三分之二，且从未完全恢复。我们表明，这些削减波及了整个美国研究体系，导致大学科学家数量、博士培养和研究产出下降。1970年受国防部资助的早期职业科学家比同龄人更有可能离开大学，许多人转向工业界。尽管其中一些人更有可能获得专利，但从总体上看，这似乎减少了相关技术中与科学关联的创新，并压缩了美国在原先由国防部驱动的领域中的领导地位。这些影响延伸至与军事目标更密切相关的结果：进入国防工业基础的博士数量减少，且在最初受冲击最大的技术领域，与科学关联的国防专利数量在短暂增加后转为长期下降。
